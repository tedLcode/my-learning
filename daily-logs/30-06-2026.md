## 📚 What I Studied Today
- Topic: Neo4j Graph Data Model for Customer Journeys
  - What I understood: The proposed graph model separates customer identity, customer state by year, and product/account ownership into distinct node types — `Person`, `Person-Year`, `Line of Business (LOB)`, `Product`, `Account Type`, `Managed Account Type` — because a customer's relationship to products changes year-over-year, and modeling that as a yearly progression node makes traversal queries much cleaner
  - What I understood: Relationships between `Person-Year` nodes represent transitions — this is what makes path analytics possible: you can traverse from one year's state to the next and ask "what changed and in what order?"
  - What I understood: Managed account types are being grouped into broader categories (Fidelity Go, Advisory Programs, SMA, FITFOLIO) to reduce model complexity without losing the business insight that matters

- Topic: FITGO Customer Path Analytics Use Cases
  - What I understood: The central business question is: what do customers do after joining FITGO? The analytics need to answer which account types are commonly added next, which journey paths lead to stronger relationships, and how workplace customers behave before and after FITGO enrollment
  - What I understood: An example journey — Workplace Customer → FITGO → Retirement Account → Additional Products — is a sequence of node transitions in the graph; finding the most common version of that sequence is a graph traversal query, not a SQL aggregation
  - What I understood: The distinction between "most frequent paths" and "most valuable paths" matters — a common path isn't necessarily the one that drives growth, so the KPIs need to capture both volume and relationship depth

- Topic: KPI Dashboard Design for Business Users
  - What I understood: Business users want customer starting point, ending point, relationship growth, account growth, popular account combinations, and most common journeys — all summarized before any deep-dive path exploration
  - What I understood: Page 1 should be KPI summaries (simple, scannable); later pages go deeper into path analytics and journey exploration — this layered structure matches how business stakeholders actually consume data
  - What I understood: Simpler, explainable visualizations are explicitly preferred over complex graph renderings — the Neo4j backend handles complexity, the UI surface should feel straightforward

- Topic: Cypher Query Language
  - What I understood: Cypher is Neo4j's query language — it uses ASCII-art-style syntax to express graph patterns (e.g., `(person)-[:HAS_YEAR]->(year)-[:JOINED]->(product)`) which reads more naturally than equivalent SQL joins across relationship tables
  - What I understood: Learning Cypher is the next concrete technical step — the team will use it to retrieve customer journey paths from the Neo4j instance once the prototype data is loaded
